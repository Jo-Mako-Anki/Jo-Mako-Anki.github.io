Grammar

  • やる - やる 【遣る】★★★★★
    to do, to undertake, to perform, to play (a game), to study
  • の - 【の】 ★★★★★
    (at sentence-end, falling tone) indicates a confident conclusion
  • ぞ - 【ぞ】★★
    (sentence end) adds force, indicates command
  • よ - 【よ】★★★★★
    indicates certainty, emphasis, contempt, request, etc.
  • あげる - あげる【上げる】 ★★★★★
    to give
  • な - 【な】★★★
    (sentence end, mainly masc.) indicates emotion or emphasis
  • くる - くる【來る】
    to come (spatially or temporally), to approach, to arrive

Lesson

テレビで　映画を　やってる！
They are showing a movie on TV.
やってる is the て-form with いる of やる.
やる means to do.
It works just like する and is very common too.

Litteraly, this sentence means "the movie is doing by way of television."


ここは　(アンキ)　のいえ
Anki’s House


わたしも　(ポケモン)を　育ててるの！
I’m raising Pokémon, too.
育てる means "to raise".
You'll see it a lot in pokemon games.

The て-form is 育てて. It's attached to いる, that has been shortened to る.
So, 育ててる, raising.

The の at the end of a sentence can be used in different ways.
It can be explanatory, or for emphasis, for asking questions.
Here's it's for giving a new fact. She's making a statement.
It's usually said with だ or です, in which case the の is shortened to ん, giving んです.


モンスターボールだ　中に　(ポケモン)が　入ってるぞ！
It’s a Poké Ball. It contains a Pokémon!
ぞ is an ending particle.
It strongly emphasizes a (usually male) speaker's emotion or conviction about something.


写真が　飾って　ある！
It’s a picture of Professor Oak.
We saw the ている form for an ongoing action.
てある is similar but it can only follow transitive verbs.


ここは　(ライバル)　のいえ
Rival’s House


あら　(アンキ)くん！
Oh! Hi, Anki!


(ライバル)の　写真だ！
It’s a picture of Rival!


(オーキド)『(ポッポ)たち　どうも　ありがとうよ！
OAK: Well, my good Pidgey, thanks for the help!
We saw the pluralising suffix turned "I" into "We" with おれ.
Here's it's used with ポッポ, the name of the pokemon.
So it applies to the 3 ポッポ in the picture.

どうも　ありがとう, you probably already know, means thank you.
Both words can be used to say thanks, so both of them together, is like "thank you very much".

The よ is an ending particle, just like ぞ.
It's used in the same way, to put emphasis on the sentence.
It strengthens the thanks in this case.
Because it's assertive, it's mostly used by men.
ぞ is like a super strong よ, so you'll only see in fiction, where よ is actually used when speaking in real life.


おかげで　おまえたちの　ことが　また　少し　わかったぞ！
I think I understand you all a bit better now, thanks to this.
This may seem complicated, but if you break it down, we already saw the grammar.

おかげ is "help". So おかげで means "by way of help".
It's a common expression to say "thank to".

おまえ means you, like きみ.
Since Oak is talking to the 3 pokemon is using たち.

こと is for abstract things.
So far, we have "thanks to your thing..." or "thanks to you."

また means again.
少し means a little.
They're both adverbs, so they apply to the verb わかる.

わかる is interesting.
It means "to become clear". 
And が marks こと. So こと is the subject and what becomes clear.
But in english we like to have pronouns as subject, so わかる is translated as "To understand."
So in english you have "I" or another pronoun, is the subject of doing the action of understanding "こと".
But in japanese こと is the subject, and doing the action of being understandable, becoming clear.

That's important to understand, because one, you don't get confused by the particle が.
Two, a lot of japanese are like this, where you don't need a person to be the subject of the sentence. It's less humancentric in a way.

So litteraly, "Thanks to you, your thing becomes a little bit more clear again."


おお　(アンキ)では　ないか！
Ah! If it isn’t Anki!
We mention before では is the て-form of だ.
Associated with the い-Adjective ない, it becomes ではない the negative form of だ.
So Oak is saying it isn't Anki.

But か is the particle that turns a statement into a question. We saw it in かな.

We a sentence in the negative form, it's looks a bit like the question tag in English: "It isn't Anki, isn't it ?".


そうだ　そうだ！
Oh, of course!
This じゃ gave me so much trouble when I started Pokemon Fire Red.
That's just Oak, or old character way of saying だ.

You remember that we used そう we an adjective to say "it appears like".

そうだ is a common expression to say "that's right", "it appears so", "that's correct". The "correct" translation really depends on the context.


今日は　おまえと　(ライバル)に　(ポケモン)を　あげるの　じゃったな！
I promised to give you and Rival your Pokémon today, didn’t I? Well, in that case, why don’t we head over to—
This is the second and last big sentence of this sentence.
But we just need to pay attention to the particles to break it down.

今日は means "as for today".

おまえと　(ライバル)に means "to you and rival".
The particle に means we are the target of the action.

(ポケモン)を.
The particle を means Pokemon is receiving the action of the verb.

あげる is the verb. It means to give.
あげる, くれる and もらう is one big family if you will.
Something is being given between two people, and the verb depends on the perspective.
あげる means the speaker is giving to the listener.

So we have "I give Pokemon to you and Rival."
That clause is turned into a noun by the particle の.
You can think of it like "the fact I give Pokemon to you and Rival."

We just saw that じゃ is actually Oak's way of saying だ.
So じゃった is だった, the past form of だ.

The な is the ending particle we saw in かな.
It can be used for a lot of different things, like suprise, hope, admiration, uncertainty, emphasis, or here to ask confirmation.

"the fact I give Pokemon to you and Rival, was today right ?"


んんッ？
Hmm?
This is a very common interjection.
Like "Huh ?!" in english.


な　なんだ！？
Wh-what’s this?!
なんだ is very very common.
Just like そうだ, the translation depends on context but it conveys a element of surprise, like "What ?!"

Notice the な at the start, it's not な it's part of なんだ.
Oak has to start the word again because he was surprised.
So just like it's translated in english: Wh-what.
It's not just with なんだ, you'll see that with any word.


あっ！　野生の　(ピカチュウ)が　飛びだしてきた！
A wild (POKEMON) appeared!
飛びだしてきた is a lot of things packed into one verb.

飛ぶ means "to fly", "to jump", "to leap."
When we are combining two verbs into one, we use the い stem of the verb, so it becomes とび instead of とぶ.
出す (だす) means "to get out", "to reveal".
So 飛びだす means "to jump out", "to suddenly appear".

You may think it's like the て-form, it's not.
飛んで出す would have been two successive actions
飛びだす is just one verb.
Using the い stem of a verb to combine it with another one is pretty common, especially with 出す.

飛びだして is the て-form of 飛びだす.
きた is the verb くる, to come, in the past form.

飛びだしてきた means "came appearing suddenly".
Something you will see everytime you meet a wild pokemon, so you'll get used to it.


やったー！　(ピカチュウ)を　捕まえた！
All right! You caught (POKEMON)!
やった is the past form of the verb やる, to do.

Japanese use the past tense in a different way that english.
It's not so much that the action was in the past, but it's that's it's finished, it's been accomplished.

So "doing" the pokemon, or hopefully "capturing" the pokemon is in past form because it's over, it's accomplished.
やった is actually used a victory cry that may have heard before.

The verb わかる is a good example of this.
If I ask you the question わかるか, to say "I understand", you don't say "わかる", but "わかった."
So why use the past when the action is happening now, or to be fair, just happened.
That's because わかる means "to become clear". 
So わかった doesn't mean that you understood something some time ago, it means that the action of becoming clear is done, finished, completed.

捕まえた is the past form of 捕まえる.
It means "to catch". 

"I did it ! I caught Pikachu!"
