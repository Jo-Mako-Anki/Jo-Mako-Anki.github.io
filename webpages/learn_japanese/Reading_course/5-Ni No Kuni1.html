Lesson

(オリバー)　急がんかい！
Get a move on, Ollie-boy!
急がん is the negative form of 急ぐ, to hurry.

かい is a sentence ending particle.
It turns a sentence into a yes or no question.

So, "You're not hurrying up, are you ?"
Which is way to say "Hurry up !"


(シズク)　待ってよ～！
Slow down, Mr. Drippy!


もうすぐ　(ゴロネール)王国や！！
But Ding Dong Dell's just down the way!
すぐ is an adverb indicating short temporal or physical distance.
It means soon.

もう is an adverb used to indicate and emphasize change of state or condition.
It can mean "already" or "not yet".

 もうすぐ is a common expression that means "Almost".

王国 is made of two easy kanji to remember.
王 is king. 国 is country or region.
王国 means Kingdom.

や is a bit special here.
It's だ in Kansai-ben dialect. 


ど　どうしよう！？
What do we do?
どうしよう is a common expression that means "What to do ?"

It's actually どう that means "how, what" and しよう, the volitional form of する.


逃げるで～！
Run for i— Aaaaaahhh!
That で after the verb 逃げる is Kansai dialect.
It depends on context, but here it means "Let's".

"Let's escape" or "Let's run".


ほな行くで　(オリバー)！
Right, let's get going, shall we?
Here the Kansai dialect once more.
行くで.
That means "Let's go."


ま　待ってよ～！！
Hey, wait for me!


これが…
So this is...


二ノ国……
...your world.


せや！　おまえらの世界とは違う　もうひとつの世界や！
That's right! A whole 'nother world. Beautiful, en't it?
せや is kansai dialect for そうだ, "That's right."

ら is a pluralizing suffix, like たち. たち is more polite.

とは is pretty advanced. It's some N1 grammar.
It's a compound particle marking an event, action or state that the speaker/writer does not expect and that causes him/her to feel some emotion.

Mr Drippy here is happy to be in his world again, whis is different (違う) than our world, the human world.

や at the end is once again the particle だ.
もう一つ means another.

So Mr Drippy is actually comparing the 2 worlds here, the human world and Ni No Kuni.


あっ　ごめんなさい
Sorry, Mister!
ごめんなさい you probably already know, means "sorry".

ごめん by itself can mean sorry. ごめんなさい is more polite.

なさい is actually the imperative form of なさる  the honorific form of する. We'll see that in different structures.


おはよう！　(レイラ)おばさん！
G'morning, Miss Leila!
おばさん means Aunt, or ma'am.
Notice the polite suffix さん.


ああ　(オリバー)　おはよう
Why, good morning, Oliver.


今日のぶんなら　そこにおいてあるよ
Your groceries are over there, honey.
ぶん means part or portion.
なら is the provisional form of だ. Think conditional or hypothetical.

The first clause means "Today's portion, if".

そこ is part of the Kosoado family.
And since you know ここ means "here", then you can figure out that そこ means "there".

おいて is the て-form of おく.
おく as a main verb means 'put' or 'place'.
The て-form of a verb plus the helping verb おく describes an action performed in preparation for something.
The action is done (or will be done) with the future in mind.

So she put our groceries "there" in advance because she knew we were coming.


うん！
Thanks!


あ　それと
Oh, I almost forgot—


昨日(マーク)が　見せたいものがあるって言ってたけど　会ったかい？
Did Philip find you? He said he had something to show you, dear.
There's a lot gong on here, let's break it down.

たい is an auxiliary adjective that means "wanted".
It connects to the い-Stem form of verbs.
Since 見せる means to show, 見せたい means "showing wanted".
So "Philip want to show".

もの means tangible thing (like a car). こと is the equivalent for abstract things.

見せたい　もの　が　ある means "there's something I want to show you".
Note that たい is only used to describe your own desire.

Which is why we have って.
It's the quoting particle, so the lady is reporting what was said.
言って is the て-form of 言う, to say. It's combined with the past form of ある, あった. It gives 言ってた, "was saying."

けど is a conjunction particle.
It means but, however, although.

The rest we know.
I'll just point out that かい is actually か and い another particles that strenghtens the question.

So we have "Yesterday, Philip, I want to show you something, he was saying, but, you met, yes or no ?"


え　ホント？
R-Really!?


じゃ　手に入ったんだ…
So he finally finished it, huh...
We mentionned it before, じゃ is the casual form of では.
It's used very often at the start of the sentence.

手 is hand.
手に入る means to obtain, to come into possession.
Litteraly, "enter hands".
If you play any jrpg, you'll this very often as it's often use when you obtain items through treasure chests for example.


それで　(マーク)はどこにいるの？
Do you know where he is right now?
どこ is part of the Kosoado family.
As other pronouns that start with the D sound, it's interegative.
どこ - Where
どれ - Which
だれ - Who

The particle の is at end of a sentence is a common way to ask a question as we already saw.


今さっき　配達に出かけたところだから
He just stepped out with some deliveries for me.
This sentence is all new stuff.
But we're not shying away from those no more.

今 means now.
さっき means a moment ago.
今さっき means just a moment ago. So it happened even closer to the present than just さっき.

配達 is delivery.

出 means coming out or to leave.
It's often used with other verbs. We saw it with 出会う.
かける is also used in a lot of different ways.
出かける means to leave, to set out to do something.

ところ as a noun means place.
When used after a verb, it means the verb is about to happen, or just happenned.

だから is used after a reason.
The next is "he's not far".
And the reason for that is this sentence: He just went out.


まだそのへんにいると思うよ
I doubt he'll have gotten far.
まだ　means still or not yet, depending on what follows.

へん is area, vicinity.
So そのへん means around here.

思う means to think.

We saw the particle と being used for quotations.
Someone said that ...
It's also used with thoughts.

So: "Still, this area, he is, I think."


ありがとう！　(レイラ)おばさん！
Thanks, Miss Leila!


あぁ　お母さんによろしくね
Oh! Say "hi" to your mother for me!
お母さん means mother.
You'll notice the polite prefix お and polite suffix さん.
It's a pattern for family members.
お兄さん, お姉ちゃん, おばさん...

よろしく is greetings.

As you can see again, に is the target particle, but the target  doesn't have to be a location, it can be person.


うん　わかった！
Sure thing, Miss Leila!


おーい！　(オリバー)！
Hey! Ollie!


こっちだ、こっち！
Over here!


あ、(マーク)！
(Oh, it's Phil!)
