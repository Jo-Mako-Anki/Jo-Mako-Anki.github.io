Pokemon Let's Go (1)

Information

Third game.
We're still going with Pokemon, revisiting the remake of Pokemon Fire-Red.
This time the text will be in kanji, but don't panic! You'll see that it actually helps spotting the particles and analyze the sentence.
I considered going with Pokemon Black, to follow the franchise chronology, put Black is harder than the other pokemons.

The first two games really served as an introduction.
This time, we're still going to review basic grammar and vocabulary but this lesson is going to be a lot longer.
Instead of having 14 and 29 new items, we now have 81.
That may seem a bit much, but I thought it would be good to stick with this game a bit longer.

Firstly because it allows to get out of the opening speech and see a bit of story and dialogue.
Secondly, it's a recent game. Modern graphics means easy to learn kanji. So we have easy sentences with good presentation.
If you find the task a bit daunting, I divided this game in two part so you can take a break in between.

Grammar

  • こと - こと【事】 ★★★★★
    thing, matter
  • てもらう -
    to get somebody to do something
  • も - 【も】★★★★★
    too, also, in addition, as well, (not) either (in a negative sentence)
  • ん - 【ん】 ★★★★

  • の - 【の】 ★★★★★
    nominalizes verbs and adjectives
  • で - 【で】
    indicates means of action, cause of effect, by
  • か - 【か】★
    indicates a question (sometimes rhetorical)
  • これ - これ【此れ】 ★★★★★

  • から - 【から】★★★
    from (e.g. time, place, numerical quantity), since
  • ている - 【てる】★★★★★
    indicates continuing action or state (e.g. to be ..ing, to have been ..ing)
  • に - 【に】★★★

  • どれ - どれ 【何れ】★★★★★

  • いる - いる 【居る】★★★★★

  • お - お【御】 ★★★
    honorific/polite/humble prefix
  • する - する 【為る】★★★★★

  • ます - 【ます】

  • どう - いかが【如何】 ★★

  • そう - 【そう】★★★★★

Lesson

はじめまして！
Hello there! Glad to meet you!


ポケットモンスターの　世界へ　ようこそ！
Welcome to the world of Pokémon!


わたしの　名前は　(オーキド)
My name is Oak.


では　はじめに　きみの　ことを　教えて　もらおう！
Now, why don’t you tell me about yourself!
We already know はじめる, to start, to begin.
はじめに means first of all.

こと and もの both mean things.
The difference is that こと is for abstract things, and もの for actual things.

もらおう is th volitional form of もらう, to receive.
The volitional form is used for making suggestions, very much like saying "Let's do x" in english.
You'll notice that in the orginal Oak was using 教えてくれ.
So he was using くれる instead of もらう.
In both case, you receive something, it's just a matter of perspective.
くれ was the imperative form, but he's now using the volitional form.
So he got more polite in the remake.

A close translation would be "Well, first of all, let's learn about your thing."


きみは　どんな　姿かな？
What do you look like?


名前も　教えて　くれるかな！
And how about a name, hmm?
も is a very important particle, and it works like は.
They're the only two particles that don't change what's happening in the sentence.
は marks the topic of the sentence.
も is used to change the topic of the sentence and it means "too" or "also".
So we're now talking about 名前.

Oak wants to know our name as well.

くれる is now used in his dictionary form.
You'll notice that each time 教える was in the て-Form because it connects the verbs.
But it's the second verb that tell us which tense is being used in the sentence.
教えてくれ: imperative
教えてくれる: non past 
押してもらう: volitional


ふむ……　きみは　(アンキ)　と　いうんだな！　　はい　　いいえ
Right... So your name is Anki ?　Yes　No
This is a bit difficult.

ふむ is easy, it's an interjection that means "hmm"; "I see".
Japanese use a lot of these interjection as a way to keep the conversation going.

と can be used to make list, but more often it will be used a quotation particle.
Litteraly "Anki" becomes アンキと in japanese.
You can put it after names, but also entire phrases or your own thoughts.

いう is to say, to call.
という is a common expression meaning "called".

So, "as for you, you're called Anki".
の can also be used as nominalizer.
It turns an entire clause into a single noun.
It's very often pronounced ん instead of の, that's not just Oak this time. It's just easier to prounounce.

We can finally see な without か this time.
We have だ before, so Oak is actually saying "as for you, you're called Anki" as a statement.
But he's not asking you, but he still wants confirmation. We use な for that.

はい means Yes, and いいえ means No.
Two words you'll use all the time in conversations but also in jrpg to confirm choices.


名前は　なんて　いうのかな？
What was his name, now?


きみの　(ライバル)の　名前は……？
What is your rival’s name?
Here is again a common practice of the particles は.
You can see we're missing the second part of the sentence.
That's because he's aking the question.

We saw that in the first lesson.

I named my rival "Rival" because I'm lazy and without imagination.
Be careful that it's not the noun "a rival", it's the name of the character.


(ライバル)　で　いいかな？　はい　いいえ
It was Rival, was it? Yes No
We have the particle で here.
It's not really a great example to understand it.
で is usually used to indicate a mean or a method.
It's kinda like what's happening here.
We're using that name for that character.


うむ　かれは　(ライバル)　と　いうのか！
Mm-hmm. I see! His name is Rival!
This is a bit difficult.

ふむ is easy, it's an interjection that means "hmm"; "I see".
Japanese use a lot of these interjection as a way to keep the conversation going.

と can be used to make list, but more often it will be used a quotation particle.
Litteraly "Anki" becomes アンキと in japanese.
You can put it after names, but also entire phrases or your own thoughts.

いう is to say, to call.
という is a common expression meaning "called".

So, "as for you, you're called Anki".
の can also be used as nominalizer.
It turns an entire clause into a single noun.
It's very often pronounced ん instead of の, that's not just Oak this time. It's just easier to prounounce.

We can finally see な without か this time.
We have だ before, so Oak is actually saying "as for you, you're called Anki" as a statement.
But he's not asking you, but he still wants confirmation. We use な for that.

はい means Yes, and いいえ means No.
Two words you'll use all the time in conversations but also in jrpg to confirm choices.


いよいよ　これから　きみの　物語の　はじまりだ！
Your very own Pokémon tale is about to unfold!
これ means "this".
It's part of the kosoado family, like ここ and この.

から is a particle that marks a starting point.
It can be a a time, location, person...

So これから means "from this", like "from this point forward." 

Litteraly: "At last, from this point forward, (it's) the start of your story."


夢と　冒険と！　ポケットモンスターの　世界へ！
Yes, a world of dreams and adventures with Pokémon awaits!


レッツ　ゴー！
Let’s go!


ニンテンドースイッチだ！　……テレビと　つながってる
It’s a Nintendo Switch! It’s connected to the TV.
Here we finally have what we could call the actual present tense.

つながる means "to be attached, to be connected".
And you should recognize the て from the て-form, つながって.
What about the る ?

It comes from いる, "to be".
Now let's says  "レッドが　いう". It means "Red says."
For the present, in english, we'd say "Red is saying".
We do the same in japanese, we use the verb いる.
As always we use the て-form to connects the two verbs.
It becomes いっている. 
Since it's bit hard to pronounce, ている is very often shortened to てる.

So つながる becomes つながって いる, shortened to つながってる.
You will see this all the time.

We saw the particle と being used for quotations and meaning "and".
Here, it's being used to mean "with".

Litteraly: "(it's) a nintendo switch! it's connecting with TV."


世界には　どれくらい　(ポケモン)が　いるのかな？
How many Pokémon are there in the world?
に is one of the most important particle.
It's the target particle. 
It's used to indicate the target of the action, usually a location, time, or a person.
Here, it's a location, 世界, the world.

It's combined with は, which doesn't change the meaning of the sentence.
It's just here for emphasis.

どれ is part of the Kosoado family.
It means which. 
くらい means approximatively.
どれくらい is an expression that means "how much, "how long", "how far".

We see いる on it's own this time.
We saw テレビがある, meaning there's a TV.
ある is used for inanimate things like TV.
いる is used for living things like Pokemon.

So, "In the world, how much, there is Pokemon".
の is the Normalizer, and かな turns it into a question.
"How many Pokémon are there in the world?"


(ライバル)『おじゃまします！
???: I’m coming up!
おじゃまします is a common polite expression that is similar "pardon my intrusion".
Japanese say it when entering people's home.

じゃま is an obstacle, nuisance, disturbance.

する is the verb "to do".
Pretty important.

Attached to する is ます, which makes a verb formal.
We saw it at the very begining with はじめまして the formal T-form of はじめる.
The formal form for はじまる is はじます.
Take out the る, add the ます.

But する is one of the only irregular veb in japanese, so する doesn't become すます but します.
You'll see する so often that you won't have any problem remembering that.

So じゃまします means "to do the nuisance".
Hence why japanese say it, they're apologizing for causing the nuisance of entering.

お is a polite prefix.
Like the noun 酒 is usually said お酒.
Gotta show some respect.

Notice the まーす instead of ます.
It indicates a trailing sound like "comiiiiing".


(ライバル)『(アンキ)　おはよ！
RIVAL: Morning, Anki!
おはよ is the abbreviated form of おはよう.

Trivia: 
It's always written in kana, but in kanji it's お早う.
早う means early or right away.
So お before we just mentionned is for politeness.

ございます is the formal form of the verb ござる which is the old super polite form of ある (to be).
Now, you know where おはようございます comes from.


……どうした？　ふしぎそうな　顔　して……
What’s going on? You’ve got one weird look on your face!
どう means "how".
した is the past form of する.
どうした is a common expression to ask "What's going on, what's happening."

"ふしぎ" means "strange, curious".
It's a な-Adjective, so there should be a な after it.
Instead we have そう.
そう means "it looks like", "it appears".
You add it to the adjective to say that something seems ... "adjective".
As we saw into the introduction, ふしぎそう can be used as a single adjective.
We put it before a noun, "顔", "face".
And そう before a noun becomes そうな.

So (pun intended) "ふしぎそうな　顔" means "A weird looking face".

して is the て form of する.
する is used in a more flexible way than "to do" in english.
Like here, "I'm doing a weird looking face"


いよいよ　今日！　オレたち　ついに！
Today’s finally the big day!
今日 means today.
It's made of two easy kanji to remember.
今: now.
日: day.

おれ means I, like 私.
There's a lot of different ways to say I in japanese. おれ is mostly used by young males.
It's written in Katakana only for stylistic reasons.
たち is a pluralizing suffix.
So おれたち means "us", or "we".

ついに is adverb, notice the に at the end that's like the "ly" in english for adverbs as well.


あッ　でも　そのまえに……
Oh, but before we do...
That "ッ" doesn't carry a lot of meaning.
It's a glotteral stop.
So that "あ" before is very short.
One of those stylish thing you'll see often.

でも means but.

前 (まえ) means before when talking about time, or in front when talking about locations.
Either way, it's usually used with the particle に.
これ means this, and since we use it with 前 that is a noun, it becomes この.
このまえに is a common expression that means "before this".


きのう　(アンキ)に　送った　メール！
You read the email I sent you yesterday, right?
昨日 (きのう) is yesterday.
It's a time, but you notice we don't use the particle に with it.
Relative expressions like yesterday, today next week, don't need the particle.
They usually are at the start of the sentence like here.
We use に for fixed times like Tuesday or 6 o'clock.
Just like in english, we need to say on Tuesday, or at 6 o'clock.

送った is the past form of 送る.
Again, notice the た.

メール is email.

アンキ takes the particles に because it's the target particle.
The email was sent to アンキ, the target.
